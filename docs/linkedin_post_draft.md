## Post Text

I work in developer experience. Last week I built a robot arm that picks up objects autonomously — not to become a robotics engineer, but to understand what AI-assisted learning actually feels like from the inside.

My teacher was Claude. My prompt:

"As an experienced physics AI developer, given that we have all the material in place for the SO-101 from LeRobot, please design a 5-day course to educate me the foundation of physics AI while tying that knowledge to the process of building the robot from scratch to inference/evaluation. Please make it an interactive session, and make sure each session can fit into one context window. Structure this folder to help track progress and log everything you need to move forward without me reminding you. Be professional, make your words easy to understand, and concise."

Five days later, the robot picked up a toy shark and placed it in a cup — on its own. (Video attached)

Here's what surprised me: the most valuable thing I learned wasn't robotics. It was experiencing AI engineering concepts I'd only read about.

When my robot reached the object 100% of the time but dropped it 20% during transport, I felt why step-by-step evaluation matters. "It sometimes fails" became "the grip is the bottleneck." That shift from vague to actionable only clicks when you live it.

The real friction was the toolchain: building frameworks from source because pre-built packages lacked GPU support, CLI arguments that silently changed between versions, SSH sessions that broke interactive tools. Pain that no AI tutor can skip over. You have to feel it — and that's exactly the developer experience insight I was after.

All of this was learnable with an AI subscription and a hardware kit. Which made me wonder — who else could learn this way?

Every day my feed is full of AI content — hype, hot takes, people selling courses that promise expertise. It's disorienting, and it can mislead people who genuinely want to learn. What I've found is much simpler: with the right prompt and a foundation model, you can design your own learning path tailored to exactly what you need to know.

I have the privilege to do this — the time, the equipment, the background to know where to start. And that worries me. AI has the potential to lower the barrier of learning for anyone eager to learn. But if it only reaches people who already have access, it just widens the gap. I'd rather it be the thing that helps someone learn what they need to meet their own goals — whatever those are.

I'll keep sharing what I learn. And maybe along the way, I'll figure out how to make this kind of learning more accessible beyond my bubble.
